Did your company/community/institution ever need to develop specific software to perform one (or more) of the following?

Store, edit and share data among and outside your organization
Manage heterogeneous and evolving types of data
Visualize data in ways specific to each department of your organization
Import/export data from/to each department of your organization
Compare multiple objects from your data
Track evolutions in your data over time

> Sounds familiar...

In-house software can be costly to develop and tedious to maintain, especially if this is not your core competence.

What if there was a free, open-source solution out there, that would be flexible enough to adapt to your needs?

Meet Tyle, a system based on free, open-source, state-of-the-art, community-maintained software.

> I'm listening...

Tyle lets engineers model their "objects" with the XML format. Because the format is very generic, it will easily adapt to any of your wildest data structures.

As data lives in plain text files, it can be stored and accessed with simple shared-folders, FTP, or even Dropbox.

No mysterious database technology is involved. No complex infrastructure is needed: A text editor is enough if you have to change a number in there. You can actually get started locally in a few minutes.

> Simple, I get it

The beauty of Tyle is data augmentation.

Once data is modelled, it can be "augmented" with any number of built-in or custom "views".

So let's say you are modelling cars. You could define views to show:

  • The car's external geometry for aerodynamics.
  • The car's structural geometry for mechanical engineering.
  • The car's kynematic model for simulations.
  • The car's performance figures for marketing.
  • etc.

Oh! And these views can be used to display one single object, or to compare multiple objects with each other.

> Looks good

Besides views, data can be augmented with importers and exporters.

As their names suggest, these allow you to map Tyle's data format to your existing databases and tools.

Writing a custom view, importer or exporter is done with Javascript, the language of the web.

You can leverage any library available out there just by declaring it as a dependency. Check-out d3.js if you want inspiration.

> Sounds useful!

Explore your data with Tyle-Explorer.

Tyle-Explorer is available in your browser (if your data is stored on a server) or as a standalone application (allowing you to also access data locally, or over FTP and SSH).

Tyle-Explorer displays your object's views and allows you to compare various objects and to run your importers/exporters.

Screenshot Tyle-Explorer.

You need to keep track of evolutions in your data in time? You need engineers to access/edit the data concurrently?

Tyle will work extremely nicely with tools like Git or Subversion, or even Dropbox!

Originally designed for source code versioning, Git will do a great job at tracking changes in your data, and merging the work done by various engineers concurrently.

Cherry-on-top: Create a GitHub account, and instantly share your data with the world for free!

You can even host Tyle-Explorer on a static page to displays your views to everyone!!

> That blows my mind!!

Without introducing any unnecessary complexity, Tyle gives your data up to five dimensions:

  • Model widely different types of data
  • Visualize your data in multiples ways
  • Compare multiple objects of the same type
  • Track evolutions of your objects in time
  • Manage parallel versions of your data
